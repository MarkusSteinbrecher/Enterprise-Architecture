# Capability 23: EA Performance Management

**Domain:** Architecture Management

**Tagline:** Measure, evaluate and demonstrate the value and effectiveness of Enterprise Architecture.

## Definition

Establishing and operating a framework to define, measure and report on the performance and value contribution of the EA function, ensuring continuous alignment with business objectives and stakeholder expectations.

## Objectives

Make the impact and value of EA visible and measurable for stakeholders. Enable data-driven improvement of EA services, processes and capabilities. Ensure accountability and transparency of the EA function.

## Tasks

1. **Define EA performance goals** aligned with business and IT strategy.
2. **Identify and establish KPIs and metrics** (e.g. stakeholder satisfaction, architecture compliance rate, time-to-decision, repository adoption).
3. **Implement measurement** and data collection mechanisms.
4. **Produce regular performance reports** and present to key stakeholders.
5. **Analyse results,** identify improvement areas and drive corrective actions.

## Inputs

- Business and IT strategy and goals
- EA capability maturity assessment results
- Stakeholder feedback and satisfaction data
- Repository usage and data quality metrics

## Outputs

- EA KPI framework and metric definitions
- Periodic EA performance reports and dashboards
- Improvement recommendations and action plans
- Stakeholder communication on EA value contribution

## Roles

- Lead Enterprise Architect (R)
- CIO / CTO (A)
- Domain Architects (C)
- EA Repository Manager (C)

## Common Issues with an Immature Capability

- EA cannot demonstrate its value, leading to budget cuts and loss of management support.
- Metrics are either not defined or too technical – they don't resonate with business stakeholders.
- Performance data is collected but never acted upon – reporting without improvement.

## Pragmatic Approach – Essentials That Are Needed for Success

- Start with 3–5 meaningful KPIs that business stakeholders care about – avoid metric overload.
- Combine quantitative metrics with qualitative feedback (e.g. stakeholder interviews).
- Report regularly and visibly – if nobody sees it, it doesn't count.
