# Capability 10: Architecture Validation

**Domain:** Architecture Development – Application Architecture

**Tagline:** Confirm that an architecture is fit for purpose and identify any gaps or issues.

## Definition

Architecture Validation involves ensuring that the developed architecture meets the defined requirements and aligns with business goals. It includes verifying the accuracy, completeness, and feasibility of the architecture.

## Objectives

Verify that architecture deliverables meet defined requirements, comply with standards and principles, and are feasible for implementation. Identify gaps, risks and issues early to avoid costly rework.

## Tasks

1. **Review architecture documentation** against requirements, principles and standards.
2. **Perform compliance assessments** against architecture policies and guidelines.
3. **Assess technical feasibility,** risks and implementation readiness.
4. **Conduct validation sessions** with key stakeholders to confirm fit for purpose.
5. **Document findings,** gaps and recommendations for remediation.

## Inputs

- Architecture documentation.
- Requirements specifications.
- Business goals and objectives.
- Guiding principles, standards, policies and guidelines

## Outputs

- Validation reports.
- Compliance assessment results.
- Updated architecture documentation.

## Roles

- Business Architect, Application Architect, Data Architect, Technology Architect (R)
- Lead Enterprise Architect (A)

## Common Issues with an Immature Capability

- Validation happens too late – issues are discovered during implementation when changes are expensive.
- No clear validation criteria – reviews are subjective and inconsistent.
- Validation is seen as a bottleneck – projects try to skip or rush through it.

## Pragmatic Approach – Essentials That Are Needed for Success

- Define clear validation criteria based on architecture principles, standards and requirements.
- Integrate validation early in the architecture process – don't wait until the end.
- Keep reviews lightweight and constructive – focus on risk-based prioritization, not bureaucracy.
