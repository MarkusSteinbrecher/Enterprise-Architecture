# Capability 8: Capability Definition

**Domain:** Architecture Development – Business Architecture

**Tagline:** Define what the organization does and how it delivers value.

## Definition

Capability Definition involves identifying and defining the core capabilities that an organization needs to achieve its business objectives. It provides a structured approach to understanding what the organization does and how it delivers value.

## Objectives

Create a clear and consistent understanding of the organization's core abilities. Enable strategic planning and align business and IT by mapping capabilities to processes, applications and technologies. Support business transformation initiatives by providing a stable reference point.

## Tasks

1. **Capability identification** through workshops and interviews and leveraging industry reference architectures
2. **Capability modelling:** define each capability in a structured way (e.g. capability map) and organize them into categories or levels (e.g. strategic, core, supporting).
3. **Capability documentation:** document each with a clear definition, description and key attributes.
4. **Capability mapping:** map each capability to architecture domains (processes, application, data, technology).
5. **Align with stakeholders** and finalise

## Inputs

- Business strategy, business objectives and operating model
- Organizational structure and value chains
- Industry reference models (e.g. BIAN, eTOM)
- Existing capability documentation (if available)

## Outputs

- Business capability map (visual representation of the business capabilities)
- Capability descriptions (including maturity assessment)
- Business capability mapping to architecture domains

## Roles

- Business Architect (R)
- Application Architect, Data Architect, Technology Architect (C)
- Lead Enterprise Architect (A)

## Common Issues with an Immature Capability

- Capabilities are defined too granularly or inconsistently, making the map unusable for strategic discussions.
- No business ownership – the capability map is created by IT and not recognized by business.
- Confusion between capabilities and processes – leading to unclear scope and duplication.

## Pragmatic Approach – Essentials That Are Needed for Success

- Start with a Level 1–2 capability map – don't go deeper until you have a validated baseline.
- Use industry reference models as a starting point – don't reinvent the wheel.
- Focus on business ownership – capabilities must be recognized and validated by business stakeholders, not just IT.
- Keep capability definitions simple and jargon-free – if business can't understand it, it won't be used.
