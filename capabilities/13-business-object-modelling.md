# Capability 13: Business Object Modelling

**Domain:** Architecture Development – Data Architecture

**Tagline:** Define and structure the key business entities and their relationships.

## Definition

Business Object Modelling involves identifying, defining, and documenting the core business entities (objects). It establishes a common vocabulary and understanding of what data represents, including attributes, relationships, and business rules, as the base for the data architecture.

## Objectives

Create a clear and consistent understanding of business entities across the organization. Establish a conceptual data model that bridges business terminology and technical data structures. Enable effective communication between business and IT stakeholders regarding data needs and requirements.

## Tasks

1. **Identify core business objects** through stakeholder workshops, process analysis, and business capability mapping (e.g., Customer, Product, Order, Contract).
2. **Define business objects,** including their purpose, key attributes, definitions, ownership.
3. **Document business rules** that govern business objects (e.g., validation rules, lifecycle states, constraints).
4. **Validate and align** business objects with stakeholders and business processes.
5. **Maintain and evolve** the business object model as the business changes.

## Inputs

- Business strategy and objectives
- Business capability model
- Process models and documentation
- Existing data models and definitions
- Stakeholder requirements

## Outputs

- Conceptual data model (business object model)
- Business object definitions and glossary
- Business rules documentation
- Business object to process/capability mapping

## Roles

- Data Architect (A)
- Business Architect (R)
- Business Analyst (C)
- Data Owner/Steward (C)
- Lead Enterprise Architect (I)

## Common Issues with an Immature Capability

- Inconsistent terminology across different departments and systems, leading to confusion about what data represents.
- Lack of clear data ownership, resulting in poor data quality and unclear accountability.
- Gap between business concepts and technical implementation.
- Missing business rules leading to data quality issues and integration problems.

## Pragmatic Approach – Essentials That Are Needed for Success

- Start top-down with critical business objects that have the highest business impact (e.g., customer, product) – conceptually, what the business needs, not how it's technically implemented.
- Use simple, business-friendly notation.
- Establish a business glossary
- Validate by engaging data owners and business experts.
- Link business objects to processes, capabilities, and applications.
